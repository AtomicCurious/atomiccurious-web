## The ridiculously simple question

Before we continue:

<CharacterCallout tone="question">
Are flamingos born pink?
</CharacterCallout>

Most people say yes.

Which makes sense.

That’s how you’ve seen them your entire life.

But no.

Flamingos are born white or pale gray.

The color comes later.

And that’s where the chain begins.

## The color isn’t theirs

Flamingos get their color from their diet.

Mainly from carotenoids found in:

- shrimp
- algae
- small aquatic organisms

Without those pigments:

they lose the color.

That’s why zoos often supplement their diet.

<CharacterCallout tone="insight">
Pink isn’t something flamingos are born with.

It’s something they absorb.
</CharacterCallout>

And that creates the next question.

## So… why are shrimp pink?

Because the color isn’t theirs either.

Many raw shrimp are:

- gray
- translucent
- almost transparent

The intense color appears when they’re cooked.

Not because the pigment suddenly appears.

Because heat reveals what was already there.

That pigment comes from microalgae.

So now the chain becomes:

- algae
- shrimp
- flamingos

Every link is real.

But this is where it stops being trivia.

## The part where you enter the chain

Microalgae don’t just produce pigments.

They also produce a huge portion of the planet’s oxygen.

Scientific estimates place their contribution somewhere between:

**50% and 70% of Earth’s atmospheric oxygen.**

They’re not the only source.

But they are one of the largest.

And probably the most invisible.

<CharacterCallout tone="insight">
One out of every two breaths you take…

could come from organisms you’ve never seen.
</CharacterCallout>

So now you’re not looking at a color anymore.

You’re looking at a chain.

## The important part

This is where precision matters.

No:

flamingos do not directly control the planet’s oxygen.

That would be an exaggeration.

Ecosystems have redundancies.

But that’s not the point.

The point is this:

<CharacterCallout tone="question">
You don’t need something massive…

to affect an entire system.
</CharacterCallout>

In salt lagoons, flamingos act as regulators.

When they disappear:

- populations shift
- water chemistry changes
- local ecosystems destabilize

The connection isn’t direct.

But it exists.

And that matters too.

## The bonus chain you probably didn’t expect

This doesn’t just happen in oceans.

It also happens in your breakfast.

Cows form real social bonds.

They have best friends.

When they stay together:

- physiological stability improves

When they’re separated:

- stress rises
- behavior changes
- milk production can decline

So the chain becomes:

- social bonds
- less stress
- more milk
- your coffee
